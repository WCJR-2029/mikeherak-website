---
title: "The Difference Between Productive Obsession and Presence"
date: "2026-07-03"
description: "More of us are replacing idle scrolling with building something of our own - a business, a craft, a second act. That's real progress. But compulsive creating and compulsive consuming can share the same root - an inability to be still."
---

For most of the last five years, the default thing to do with a spare minute was consume. Pick up the phone, open the feed, let it pull. Mindlessly wander the web. Most of us know that reflex and have made an uneasy peace with disliking it.

Something feels like it has shifted in the last year or two. A lot of people I know have stopped mostly consuming and started making things of their own. One is building a business on the side of a full-time job. Another walked away from a steady career to start over at something harder. Some are creating - art, music, writing - and some are learning a craft or a technology they had always assumed passed them by.

I am one of them. On most mornings I am far more likely to be deep in something I am building than lost in a feed, and it feels like progress.

That sentence turns on a single word: "feels." When I am honest about what is happening underneath, the two behaviors are closer than I would like. Consuming and creating are both answers to the same question: what do I do with this restlessness? One answer is socially worthless and I feel bad about it, like I should always be productive. The other is socially rewarded and I feel good about it. But the underlying motion is the same. Neither one is stillness; both are a way of not being alone with my own attention.

Productive obsession is the harder of the two to catch, because there's an expected payoff. No one congratulates you for the fourth hour of scrolling through your favorite social media platform. Everyone congratulates you for the thing you created, or built, or launched. The output is real and the skill is real, so the compulsion gets to hide inside something genuinely good. It took me a while to notice that I sometimes reach for a project not because the work called for it, but because the moment had gone quiet and I did not know how to sit in it.

Stillness is not the absence of activity. I am not arguing for doing nothing, and I am not romanticizing the empty afternoon (though I do look forward to both at times). The people I admire most are relentless makers. The difference is not how much they do, but whether they can put it down.

I know what presence feels like when I actually land in it. Sitting in a rehearsal hall with the local orchestra, watching a section of string players move their bows in the same instant. A quiet morning with rain on the window and nowhere I have to be. A walk where I am actually on the walk and not narrating the next task in my head. None of it produces anything, and that is the point. Presence is the one category of experience that refuses to be optimized, and it is the first thing to go when the restlessness takes over.

So I have stopped asking whether I am building or consuming. That question flatters the builder and lets me off the hook. The better question is simpler and harder to answer honestly: when the moment goes still, can I stay in it, or do I insist on filling it?

I have not solved this. I am writing it down because I catch myself on the wrong side of it more often than I would like to admit. But naming it has changed something small. The next time the quiet arrives and my hand goes looking for the keyboard, I want to at least notice the reach.
